Recap: a visible Markov chain (Day 7)

A Markov chain has states and a transition matrix \(A\) whose rows sum to 1. The Markov property: the next state depends only on the current one.

Example over DNA bases — suppose we modelled which base follows which:

\[ A=\begin{array}{c c} & \begin{array}{cccc} \ \ A\ \ & \ \ C\ \ & \ \ G\ \ & \ \ T\ \ \end{array}\\ \begin{array}{c} A\\ C\\ G\\ T \end{array} & \left[\begin{array}{cccc} 0.3 & 0.2 & 0.3 & 0.2\\ 0.2 & 0.3 & 0.3 & 0.2\\ 0.2 & 0.3 & 0.3 & 0.2\\ 0.3 & 0.2 & 0.2 & 0.3 \end{array}\right] \end{array} \]

Crucially, in this chain the state IS the letter you read. You observe the state directly.

Remind them of the day-seven setup: a transition matrix with rows summing to one, and the one-step update being a matrix-vector product. Here I have made the states be the DNA letters themselves, so there is nothing hidden — you read the sequence, you know the state. Hold onto that, because the whole point of today is to break it: the states will become things we cannot read off directly.

The biology: where is the CpG island?

Genomes are not uniform. Some stretches are GC-rich (“CpG-island-like”, often near gene starts); the rest is normal background.

You are handed a sequence:

... A T G C G C G C G G C C G C A T A T A A T T A C A ...
        |________ GC-rich? ________|  |__ normal? __|

You can see the letters (A, C, G, T). You cannot see the label “GC-rich” vs “normal” — that label is the biology you actually want.

The letters are emissions. The hidden region label is the hidden state.

This is the motivating pairing for both days. A CpG island is a genomic region unusually rich in C and G; they often sit at promoters and matter for gene regulation. The key observation: nature does not annotate the sequence for you. You see A, C, G, T; you must infer which segments are islands. So there are two layers — a hidden layer of region labels and a visible layer of bases — and that is precisely a hidden Markov model.

Why the visible chain is not enough

If the label “GC-rich vs normal” were written in the sequence, we would just read it — a plain Markov chain over labels (Day 7).

But it is not written down. We only get the letters. Two different hidden states can emit the same letter:

  • A “GC-rich” region emits a G often.
  • A “normal” region emits a G too, just less often.

So a single G does not tell you the state. The state is hidden; we must infer it from the pattern of emissions over a whole stretch.

Hammer the limitation. A visible chain assumes you see the states. Here you do not, and the emissions are ambiguous: every base can come from either state, only with different probabilities. One letter is nearly useless; it is the run of letters that betrays the hidden state. That inference problem is what the HMM machinery is built to solve.

Two layers: the generative picture

An HMM tells a generative story in two coupled layers:

hidden:   z1  ->  z2  ->  z3  ->  z4     (a Markov chain: GC-rich / normal)
           |       |       |       |
           v       v       v       v
visible:  x1      x2      x3      x4      (emitted bases: A C G T)
  • The hidden layer is a Day-7 Markov chain over states (here: GC-rich, normal), governed by transition matrix \(A\).
  • At each hidden state, you draw one emission (a base) from that state’s emission distribution, governed by matrix \(B\).

You only ever see the bottom row. The top row is hidden.

This diagram is the mental model for the whole topic. Top row: the hidden chain hops between GC-rich and normal according to A, exactly the Markov chain we already know. Bottom row: every time we land in a hidden state, that state “speaks” a letter, drawn from its own emission distribution B. The arrows down are emissions; the arrows across are transitions. We observe only the bottom row and must reason backwards to the top.

The four ingredients of an HMM

  1. Hidden states — the things you cannot observe. Here: \(\{\text{GC-rich},\ \text{normal}\}\).
  2. Transition matrix \(A\) — how the hidden chain moves, rows sum to 1. \(A_{ij}=P(z_{t+1}=j \mid z_t=i)\). This is the Day-7 Markov chain.
  3. Emission matrix \(B\) — what each hidden state emits, rows sum to 1. \(B_{ik}=P(x_t=k \mid z_t=i)\) (one row per state, one column per symbol).
  4. Initial distribution \(\boldsymbol{\pi}\) — where the chain starts, \(\pi_i=P(z_1=i)\), entries sum to 1.

An HMM is the triple \((A, B, \boldsymbol{\pi})\) plus the lists of states and symbols.

List the four ingredients and keep emphasising which is old and which is new. A and pi are pure Markov-chain objects from day seven. The genuinely new piece is B, the emission matrix: for every hidden state, a probability distribution over the symbols you can observe. Note both A and B have rows that sum to one, but they are different shapes — A is states-by-states, B is states-by-symbols.

A tiny concrete 2-state HMM

States \(\{H, L\}\) — H = “GC-rich (high)”, L = “normal (low)”. Symbols: the bases \(\{A, C, G, T\}\).

\[ \boldsymbol{\pi}=\begin{bmatrix} 0.5 \\ 0.5 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad A=\begin{array}{cc} & \begin{array}{cc} H & L \end{array}\\ \begin{array}{c} H\\ L \end{array} & \left[\begin{array}{cc} 0.8 & 0.2 \\ 0.3 & 0.7 \end{array}\right] \end{array} \]

\[ B=\begin{array}{c} \begin{array}{cccc} \ A\ & \ C\ & \ G\ & \ T\ \end{array}\\ \begin{array}{c} \end{array} \left[\begin{array}{cccc} 0.1 & 0.4 & 0.4 & 0.1 \\ 0.3 & 0.2 & 0.2 & 0.3 \end{array}\right] \end{array} \begin{array}{c} H\\ L \end{array} \]

Read \(B\): state \(H\) emits C or G with prob 0.4 each (GC-rich!); state \(L\) spreads probability more evenly.

This is the example we will compute with by hand, and again in R tomorrow, so introduce it carefully. Pi says we start in either state with equal chance. A says both states are sticky — H stays H with probability 0.8, L stays L with 0.7 — which encodes that islands and background come in runs. B is the new object: the high state loves C and G, the low state is roughly uniform. Sanity check every row sums to one.

The generative story, step by step

To generate a sequence of length 3 from this HMM:

  1. Draw \(z_1\) from \(\boldsymbol{\pi}\). Say \(z_1 = H\).
  2. Emit \(x_1\) from row \(H\) of \(B\). Say \(x_1 = G\) (prob 0.4).
  3. Move: draw \(z_2\) from row \(H\) of \(A\). Say \(z_2 = H\) (prob 0.8).
  4. Emit \(x_2\) from row \(H\) of \(B\). Say \(x_2 = C\) (prob 0.4).
  5. Move: draw \(z_3\) from row \(H\) of \(A\). Say \(z_3 = L\) (prob 0.2).
  6. Emit \(x_3\) from row \(L\) of \(B\). Say \(x_3 = A\) (prob 0.3).

We observe only \(\mathbf{x} = (G, C, A)\). The path \(\mathbf{z} = (H, H, L)\) is hidden.

Walk through generation as a recipe, alternating transition and emission. Start state from pi, emit, transition, emit, transition, emit. Each choice has a probability attached. The student sees only the emitted letters G C A; the path H H L is invisible. The next slide multiplies all those probabilities together, which is the single most important by-hand skill for the exam.

Probability of ONE path with its emissions

The HMM is a product of independent choices, so multiply along the story. For path \(\mathbf{z}=(H,H,L)\) and observations \(\mathbf{x}=(G,C,A)\):

\[ P(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{z}) = \underbrace{\pi_H}_{\text{start}}\, \underbrace{B_{H,G}}_{\text{emit}}\, \underbrace{A_{H,H}}_{\text{move}}\, \underbrace{B_{H,C}}_{\text{emit}}\, \underbrace{A_{H,L}}_{\text{move}}\, \underbrace{B_{L,A}}_{\text{emit}} \]

\[ = 0.5 \times 0.4 \times 0.8 \times 0.4 \times 0.2 \times 0.3 = 0.003840. \]

The pattern: start prob → (emit, transition, emit, transition, …, emit). One \(\boldsymbol{\pi}\), then alternate \(B\) and \(A\), ending on an emission.

This is THE formula to memorise. The joint probability of a hidden path and its observations is just the product of: the initial probability, then for each position the emission probability, and between positions the transition probability. Count the factors: for length T you get one pi, T emissions, and T minus one transitions. Do this multiplication slowly; on the exam this is where marks are won. Note the answer is small — products of probabilities shrink fast, which is why real implementations work in log space.

Three classic problems

Given an HMM and an observed sequence \(\mathbf{x}\), three questions:

  1. Likelihood — how probable is \(\mathbf{x}\) under the model, \(P(\mathbf{x})\)? Summed over all hidden paths. → Forward algorithm.
  2. Decoding — what is the single most likely hidden path \(\mathbf{z}^\*\)? → Viterbi algorithm. (This is what predicts the islands.)
  3. Learning — given only unlabelled \(\mathbf{x}\), estimate \((A, B, \boldsymbol{\pi})\). → Baum–Welch (an EM algorithm).

Today: the concepts. Tomorrow: all three in R.

Frame the three problems as three different questions you might ask of the same model and data. Likelihood scores the whole model; you need it to compare models. Decoding gives you the annotation — the most probable sequence of hidden labels — which is literally the predicted island map. Learning is the hardest: estimating the parameters when you have only raw sequence and no labels. Name the algorithm for each but do not derive them; that is beyond scope. The R functions tomorrow are forward, viterbi, baumWelch.

Why summing over paths matters (likelihood)

The path \((H,H,L)\) is one way to produce \(\mathbf{x}=(G,C,A)\). But \((H,H,H)\), \((L,H,L)\), … could also produce it.

The likelihood of the data is the sum over every hidden path:

\[ P(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{\text{all paths } \mathbf{z}} P(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{z}). \]

For 2 states and length 3 that is \(2^3 = 8\) paths — fine by hand. For a real chromosome (\(2^{\text{millions}}\) paths) it is hopeless by brute force; the forward algorithm does it efficiently by reusing partial sums.

Stress the distinction between the joint probability of a specific path, which we just computed, and the likelihood of the data, which marginalises over all possible paths. The naive sum has a number of terms exponential in the sequence length, so you cannot enumerate it for a genome. The forward algorithm is the clever bookkeeping that collapses this to a linear-time computation by carrying forward partial probabilities — conceptually like reusing work, not unlike the matrix iterations from day eight. We will only call it, not implement it.

Decoding: the most likely path (Viterbi)

Decoding asks for the single best hidden path:

\[ \mathbf{z}^\* = \arg\max_{\mathbf{z}} \; P(\mathbf{z}\mid \mathbf{x}). \]

The Viterbi algorithm finds it efficiently (same trick as forward, but keeping the best path instead of the total).

For islands: run Viterbi, then read off the runs of H — those are your predicted CpG islands. This turns a probabilistic model into a concrete genome annotation.

Decoding is the payoff for biology. Viterbi returns one hidden-state label per position; wherever it says H you have a predicted GC-rich segment, and the maximal runs of H are your predicted islands. Mechanically Viterbi is the same dynamic program as forward but takes a max instead of a sum and remembers which choice gave it, so it can trace the best path back. Tomorrow the viterbi function does this in one line.

Learning: where do A, B, π come from?

So far we assumed the numbers in \(A, B, \boldsymbol{\pi}\). Where do they come from?

  • If you have labelled data (sequences with known island annotations): just count transitions and emissions and normalise. Easy.
  • If you only have raw sequence (no labels — the usual case): Baum–Welch iterates “guess the hidden states, re-estimate the parameters, repeat” until it converges. It is the EM algorithm for HMMs.

Caveat: Baum–Welch finds a local optimum — the starting guess matters, and results need a sanity check.

Address the obvious question: who told us the matrices? With labelled data, parameter estimation is just counting and normalising, like estimating any conditional probability. The realistic case has no labels, and Baum-Welch — an expectation-maximisation method — alternates between softly inferring the hidden states and re-fitting the parameters. Flag two practical warnings now so they are not surprised tomorrow: it only finds a local optimum, and it is sensitive to initialisation.

Summary

  • A genome’s island vs background label is hidden; you see only the bases. That is exactly an HMM.
  • An HMM = hidden states + transition matrix \(A\) (the Day-7 chain) + emission matrix \(B\) + initial distribution \(\boldsymbol{\pi}\).
  • Generative story: start from \(\boldsymbol{\pi}\), then alternate emit (\(B\)) and move (\(A\)). The probability of one path-with-emissions is the product of those factors.
  • Three problems: likelihood (forward), decoding (Viterbi), learning (Baum–Welch).
