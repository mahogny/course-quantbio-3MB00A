Solving linear equation systems

Day 1 — Quantitative biology

Quantitative biology and integrative omics

About this lecture

Today we start the linear-algebra block. By the end you will be able to:

  • Recognise when a biology problem is a system of linear equations.
  • Write such a system in the standard form \(A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}\).
  • Solve small \(2\times 2\) and \(3\times 3\) systems by hand using substitution and Gaussian elimination.
  • Check a candidate solution by plugging it back in.

No prior linear algebra is assumed. We build the ideas from a concrete example and keep all numbers small enough to do with pen and paper.

Welcome to day one of the linear-algebra block. This is the foundation for everything that follows — eigenvalues, Markov chains, PCA — so we go slowly. Today is deliberately by-hand and concept-heavy: the exam is written and on-campus, so you need to be able to solve a small system without a computer. A biology problem: what is in the mixture?

A tumour biopsy is not one cell type. It is a mixture: some tumour cells, some immune cells, some healthy tissue. Suppose we already know two marker readouts per cell type (say, expression of gene G1 and gene G2), measured per “unit” of each cell type:

Tumour Immune
Gene G1 signal 2 1
Gene G2 signal 1 3

We sequence a bulk sample and measure total signal: G1 \(= 8\), G2 \(= 9\).

Question: how many units of tumour (\(x_1\)) and immune (\(x_2\)) cells were in the sample?

This is the motivating pairing for the whole first two days: cell-type deconvolution. A bulk measurement is a sum over the cell types present. If we know the per-cell-type signature, recovering the amounts is exactly solving a linear system. Keep the numbers tiny here on purpose — we will make them realistic in R tomorrow.

Turning the biology into equations

Each measured signal is the sum of contributions from each cell type:

\[ \begin{aligned} \underbrace{2x_1 + 1x_2}_{\text{total G1}} &= 8\\[2pt] \underbrace{1x_1 + 3x_2}_{\text{total G2}} &= 9 \end{aligned} \]

Two unknowns (\(x_1, x_2\)), two equations. This is a system of linear equations.

“Linear” means every unknown appears only to the first power — no \(x_1^2\), no \(x_1 x_2\), no \(\sin x_1\). Just numbers times unknowns, added up.

Stress what “linear” rules out: no squares, no products of unknowns, no nonlinear functions. Each equation is a weighted sum of the unknowns set equal to a number. Almost every method in this course reduces, at its core, to solving or approximately solving a system like this.

The standard form \(A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}\)

We separate the known coefficients from the unknowns and the measurements:

\[ \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}}_{A} \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix}}_{\mathbf{x}} = \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} 8 \\ 9 \end{bmatrix}}_{\mathbf{b}} \]

  • \(A\) — the coefficient matrix. Here, the cell-type signatures (one column per cell type, one row per gene).
  • \(\mathbf{x}\) — the unknowns we want (the cell-type amounts).
  • \(\mathbf{b}\) — the right-hand side, our measurements.

The first row of \(A\) times \(\mathbf{x}\) reproduces the first equation; the second row gives the second. This packaging is what lets us scale to hundreds of genes later.

Introduce the names carefully: coefficient matrix A, unknown vector x, right-hand side b. Notation conventions for the course: uppercase for matrices, bold lowercase for vectors. Walk through how “row of A times x” rebuilds each equation — this is the first glimpse of matrix-vector multiplication, which we formalise on day 4.

Strategy 1 — Substitution

Solve one equation for one unknown, then substitute into the other.

From equation 1: \(\;2x_1 + x_2 = 8 \;\Rightarrow\; x_2 = 8 - 2x_1\).

Put that into equation 2:

\[ x_1 + 3(8 - 2x_1) = 9 \;\Rightarrow\; x_1 + 24 - 6x_1 = 9 \;\Rightarrow\; -5x_1 = -15 \]

So \(x_1 = 3\), and then \(x_2 = 8 - 2(3) = 2\).

Answer: 3 units of tumour, 2 units of immune cells.

Substitution is the method most students already half-remember from school. Do it slowly. Isolate one variable, push it into the other equation, you now have one equation in one unknown. Solve, then back-substitute. The arithmetic is the only place to slip up, so go step by step on the board.

Always check your answer

Plug \(x_1 = 3,\; x_2 = 2\) back into the original equations:

\[ \begin{aligned} 2(3) + 1(2) &= 6 + 2 = 8 \quad\checkmark\\ 1(3) + 3(2) &= 3 + 6 = 9 \quad\checkmark \end{aligned} \]

Both hold, so the solution is correct.

Checking costs seconds and catches almost every arithmetic mistake. On the exam, always do it.

Make checking a habit and say so explicitly. Substituting back into the original equations is independent of how you got the answer, so it catches sign errors and arithmetic slips. This is free marks on an exam and free peace of mind in real analysis.

Strategy 2 — Gaussian elimination

Substitution gets messy with three or more unknowns. Gaussian elimination is the systematic method that scales (it is what R uses internally).

The idea: add multiples of one equation to another to eliminate a variable, until the system is easy to read off.

Two moves are allowed (they never change the solution):

  1. Multiply an equation by a non-zero number.
  2. Add a multiple of one equation to another.

(A third, swapping two equations, just reorders them.)

Frame Gaussian elimination as “substitution made systematic”. The allowed operations are the row operations; emphasise that each one preserves the solution set, which is why we are allowed to do them. This is the algorithm behind solve() in R, so understanding it by hand demystifies the computer.

Gaussian elimination on our 2×2

Start:

\[ \begin{aligned} 2x_1 + 1x_2 &= 8 \quad(\text{R1})\\ 1x_1 + 3x_2 &= 9 \quad(\text{R2}) \end{aligned} \]

Eliminate \(x_1\) from R2. Replace R2 by \(\;\text{R2} - \tfrac{1}{2}\text{R1}\):

\[ \Big(1 - \tfrac{1}{2}\cdot 2\Big)x_1 + \Big(3 - \tfrac{1}{2}\cdot 1\Big)x_2 = 9 - \tfrac{1}{2}\cdot 8 \;\Rightarrow\; 0\,x_1 + 2.5\,x_2 = 5 \]

Now R2 says \(2.5\,x_2 = 5 \Rightarrow x_2 = 2\). Back-substitute into R1: \(2x_1 + 2 = 8 \Rightarrow x_1 = 3\). Same answer as before. \(\checkmark\)

Show the multiplier choice: we want to kill x1 in R2, R2 has coefficient 1 and R1 has coefficient 2, so subtract half of R1. The x1 term vanishes by design. Then the last equation has a single unknown — solve it, then back-substitute upward. Reassure them it gives the same answer as substitution; it is the same maths, organised.

The augmented matrix shorthand

We do not need to keep writing \(x_1, x_2\). Track only the numbers in an augmented matrix \([\,A \mid \mathbf{b}\,]\):

\[ \left[\begin{array}{cc|c} 2 & 1 & 8 \\ 1 & 3 & 9 \end{array}\right] \xrightarrow{\;\text{R2}\,-\,\frac12\text{R1}\;} \left[\begin{array}{cc|c} 2 & 1 & 8 \\ 0 & 2.5 & 5 \end{array}\right] \]

The bottom row reads \(2.5\,x_2 = 5\). The goal is this upper-triangular (“staircase”) shape: zeros below the diagonal. Then solve bottom-up.

The augmented matrix is bookkeeping: a vertical bar separates A from b, and we apply the same row operations to the whole row including the b entry. Target shape is upper triangular — once you are there, the last row is one equation in one unknown and you climb back up. This notation is essential for the 3x3 case.

Scaling up: a 3×3 system

Three cell types (tumour, immune, stroma), three marker genes. Signatures and measurements give:

\[ \begin{aligned} 1x_1 + 1x_2 + 1x_3 &= 6\\ 0x_1 + 2x_2 + 1x_3 &= 7\\ 2x_1 + 1x_2 + 1x_3 &= 8 \end{aligned} \qquad \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 6\\ 0 & 2 & 1 & 7\\ 2 & 1 & 1 & 8 \end{array}\right] \]

We eliminate column by column, top-left to bottom-right.

Now the payoff for systematic elimination. With three unknowns substitution becomes error-prone, but elimination is just “clear column 1, then clear column 2”. The first equation conveniently already has a 1 in the top-left, our pivot. Walk through the plan before doing the arithmetic.

3×3 elimination — clear column 1

Pivot is the \(1\) in the top-left. R2 already has a \(0\) there. Clear R3 with \(\;\text{R3} - 2\,\text{R1}\):

\[ \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 6\\ 0 & 2 & 1 & 7\\ 2 & 1 & 1 & 8 \end{array}\right] \xrightarrow{\;\text{R3}-2\text{R1}\;} \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 6\\ 0 & 2 & 1 & 7\\ 0 & -1 & -1 & -4 \end{array}\right] \]

Column 1 now has zeros below the pivot.

R3 minus two times R1: 2 minus 2 is 0, 1 minus 2 is minus 1, 1 minus 2 is minus 1, and 8 minus 12 is minus 4. Do each subtraction out loud. The point is that the first unknown is now confined to the first equation only.

3×3 elimination — clear column 2

Pivot is now the \(2\) in R2. Clear R3 with \(\;\text{R3} + \tfrac{1}{2}\text{R2}\):

\[ \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 6\\ 0 & 2 & 1 & 7\\ 0 & -1 & -1 & -4 \end{array}\right] \xrightarrow{\;\text{R3}+\frac12\text{R2}\;} \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 6\\ 0 & 2 & 1 & 7\\ 0 & 0 & -0.5 & -0.5 \end{array}\right] \]

Now upper-triangular. The bottom row reads \(-0.5\,x_3 = -0.5\).

R2 has minus 1 in column 2 below the pivot 2, so add half of R2 to R3 to kill it. Minus 1 plus 1 is 0; minus 1 plus a half is minus a half; minus 4 plus 3.5 is minus a half. We have reached the staircase shape. Now we read off and back- substitute.

3×3 — back-substitution

From the bottom up:

\[ \begin{aligned} -0.5\,x_3 &= -0.5 &&\Rightarrow\; x_3 = 1\\ 2x_2 + x_3 &= 7 &&\Rightarrow\; 2x_2 + 1 = 7 \;\Rightarrow\; x_2 = 3\\ x_1 + x_2 + x_3 &= 6 &&\Rightarrow\; x_1 + 3 + 1 = 6 \;\Rightarrow\; x_1 = 2 \end{aligned} \]

Solution: \(x_1 = 2,\; x_2 = 3,\; x_3 = 1\).

Check in the original: \(2+3+1=6\,\checkmark\), \(0+6+1=7\,\checkmark\), \(4+3+1=8\,\checkmark\).

Back-substitution always starts at the last row, which has one unknown. Solve it, carry that value up into the second-last row which now has one remaining unknown, and so on. Then check all three original equations. This is the full recipe they need for the exam.

The same idea in R (preview)

Tomorrow we let the computer do the elimination. The code is short:

A <- matrix(c(1, 1, 1,
              0, 2, 1,
              2, 1, 1), nrow = 3, byrow = TRUE)
b <- c(6, 7, 8)
solve(A, b)
# [1] 2 3 1

solve() runs Gaussian elimination internally — exactly what you just did by hand, but on any size.

A quick reassurance and a hook into day two. We will not type matrices by hand forever; solve does the elimination for us. But knowing what it does is what lets you trust it, debug it, and answer exam questions where no computer is allowed. Note byrow = TRUE so the matrix reads like the page.

Summary

  • A linear system = weighted sums of unknowns set equal to numbers.
  • Standard form: \(A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}\) (\(A\) coefficients, \(\mathbf{x}\) unknowns, \(\mathbf{b}\) measurements).
  • Substitution for tiny systems; Gaussian elimination (via the augmented matrix) for anything bigger — eliminate to upper-triangular, then back-substitute.
  • Always check by substituting back into the original equations.
  • Biology hook: recovering cell-type amounts from bulk measurements.
