Warm-up: transpose, identity, symmetry

Goal

  • Write the transpose \(A^\top\) of a small matrix by flipping rows and columns.
  • Recognise the identity matrix \(I\) and see that \(IA = A\).
  • Recognise when a matrix is symmetric (\(A = A^\top\)).

The rule

Transpose. \(A^\top\) turns rows into columns. The entry in row \(i\), column \(j\) of \(A\) becomes row \(j\), column \(i\) of \(A^\top\). The diagonal stays put; everything else reflects across it.

\[ A=\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad A^\top=\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 \\ 2 & 4 \end{bmatrix}. \]

Transposing twice gives back the original: \((A^\top)^\top = A\).

Identity. \(I\) has 1s on the diagonal and 0s everywhere else. Multiplying by it changes nothing: \(IA = A\) and \(AI = A\).

\[ I=\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}. \]

Symmetric. A matrix is symmetric when it equals its own transpose: \(A = A^\top\). In picture form, it is a mirror image across the diagonal — the entry at row \(i\), col \(j\) equals the entry at row \(j\), col \(i\).

\[ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 5 \\ 5 & 9 \end{bmatrix}\text{ is symmetric}, \qquad \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 5 \\ 2 & 9 \end{bmatrix}\text{ is not.} \]

Drills

Write the transpose:

  1. \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix}^\top\)

  2. \(\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 5 \\ 7 & 0 \end{bmatrix}^\top\)

  3. \(\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix}^\top\)

  4. \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \end{bmatrix}^\top\) (a row becomes a column)

  5. \(\begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 5 \\ 6 \end{bmatrix}^\top\) (a column becomes a row)

  6. \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \end{bmatrix}^\top\)

Transpose twice (\((A^\top)^\top = A\)):

  1. Take \(A=\begin{bmatrix} 8 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}\). Write \(A^\top\), then transpose that result. What do you get back?

Multiply by the identity:

  1. \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 6 & 7 \\ 8 & 9 \end{bmatrix}\)

  2. \(\begin{bmatrix} 4 & 5 \\ 6 & 7 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\)

  3. \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 9 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix}\)

  4. Without computing fully: what is \(I\,\mathbf{x}\) when \(\mathbf{x}=\begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 1 \\ 5 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(I\) is \(3\times 3\)?

Is this matrix symmetric? (yes/no, and say why):

  1. \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 3 \end{bmatrix}\)

  2. \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix}\)

  3. \(\begin{bmatrix} 5 & 0 \\ 0 & 7 \end{bmatrix}\)

  4. \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 4 & 0 \\ 4 & 2 & 5 \\ 0 & 5 & 3 \end{bmatrix}\)

  5. \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 4 & 0 \\ 4 & 2 & 5 \\ 0 & 6 & 3 \end{bmatrix}\)

A little harder:

  1. Fill in the missing entry so that \(\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 7 \\ \square & 0 \end{bmatrix}\) is symmetric.

  2. Is the identity matrix \(I\) symmetric? Explain in one line.

Check in R

Transpose is t. The identity is built with diag. To test symmetry, compare a matrix with its transpose using all and ==, or use isSymmetric.

A <- matrix(c(1, 2, 3, 4), nrow = 2, byrow = TRUE)
t(A)
#      [,1] [,2]
# [1,]    1    3
# [2,]    2    4
diag(3)            # the 3x3 identity
#      [,1] [,2] [,3]
# [1,]    1    0    0
# [2,]    0    1    0
# [3,]    0    0    1
S <- matrix(c(1, 2, 2, 3), nrow = 2, byrow = TRUE)
all(S == t(S))     # TRUE  -> symmetric
# [1] TRUE
isSymmetric(S)
# [1] TRUE

Hints

Hint 1 — flip across the diagonal

To transpose, leave the diagonal entries alone and swap each off-diagonal pair: the entry at (row 1, col 2) trades places with (row 2, col 1).

Hint 2 — rows become columns

A \(1\times 3\) row turns into a \(3\times 1\) column, and vice versa. The numbers stay in the same order; only their orientation changes.

Hint 3 — the identity changes nothing

\(I A = A\) and \(A I = A\). You do not need to compute — the answer is just the other matrix, unchanged.

Hint 4 — symmetry test

Compare the entry at (row \(i\), col \(j\)) with the one at (row \(j\), col \(i\)). If every such pair is equal, the matrix is symmetric. A single mismatch means it is not.

Step-by-step solutions

Solutions

Drill 1. Diagonal \(1,4\) stays; swap the off-diagonal \(2\) and \(3\). \[\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 \\ 2 & 4 \end{bmatrix}.\]

Drill 2. Diagonal \(0,0\) stays; swap \(5\) (row 1, col 2) and \(7\) (row 2, col 1). \[\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 7 \\ 5 & 0 \end{bmatrix}.\]

Drill 3. Off-diagonal entries are both \(0\), so swapping changes nothing. \[\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix}.\] (This is a diagonal matrix; diagonal matrices are their own transpose.)

Drill 4. A \(1\times 3\) row becomes a \(3\times 1\) column. \[\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}.\]

Drill 5. A \(3\times 1\) column becomes a \(1\times 3\) row. \[\begin{bmatrix} 4 & 5 & 6 \end{bmatrix}.\]

Drill 6. A \(2\times 3\) matrix becomes \(3\times 2\). Row 1 \((1,2,3)\) becomes column 1; row 2 \((4,5,6)\) becomes column 2. \[\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 4 \\ 2 & 5 \\ 3 & 6 \end{bmatrix}.\]

Drill 7. \(A=\begin{bmatrix}8&1\\0&2\end{bmatrix}\). First transpose: \(A^\top=\begin{bmatrix}8&0\\1&2\end{bmatrix}\). Transpose again: \((A^\top)^\top=\begin{bmatrix}8&1\\0&2\end{bmatrix}=A\). You get the original matrix back, illustrating \((A^\top)^\top = A\).

Drill 8. Multiplying by the identity on the left does nothing: \[\begin{bmatrix} 6 & 7 \\ 8 & 9 \end{bmatrix}.\]

Drill 9. Multiplying by the identity on the right does nothing: \[\begin{bmatrix} 4 & 5 \\ 6 & 7 \end{bmatrix}.\]

Drill 10. The \(3\times 3\) identity times the column leaves it unchanged: \[\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 9 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix}.\]

Drill 11. \(I\,\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{x}\), so the answer is \(\begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 1 \\ 5 \end{bmatrix}\) — the identity leaves any vector unchanged.

Drill 12. Off-diagonal pair: (row 1, col 2) \(=2\) and (row 2, col 1) \(=2\). Equal, so yes, symmetric.

Drill 13. (row 1, col 2) \(=2\) but (row 2, col 1) \(=3\). Not equal, so no, not symmetric.

Drill 14. Off-diagonal entries are both \(0\). They match, so yes, symmetric (every diagonal matrix is symmetric).

Drill 15. Check the three off-diagonal pairs: \((1,2)\!=\!4=(2,1)\); \((1,3)\!=\!0=(3,1)\); \((2,3)\!=\!5=(3,2)\). All match, so yes, symmetric.

Drill 16. Check pairs: \((2,3)=5\) but \((3,2)=6\). They differ, so no, not symmetric.

Drill 17. For symmetry, (row 2, col 1) must equal (row 1, col 2) \(=7\). So \(\square = 7\): \[\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 7 \\ 7 & 0 \end{bmatrix}.\]

Drill 18. Yes. \(I\) has all off-diagonal entries equal to \(0\), so flipping across the diagonal changes nothing: \(I^\top = I\).
