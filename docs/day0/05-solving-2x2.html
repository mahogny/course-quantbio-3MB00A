Warm-up: solving 2×2 systems

Goal

  • Get fast and confident at solving two equations in two unknowns, \(x\) and \(y\).
  • Learn two methods: substitution and elimination — and see they give the same answer.
  • Build the habit of showing every step. The written exam rewards the method, not just the final number.

The method

A 2×2 linear system looks like

\[ \begin{aligned} a_{11}\,x + a_{12}\,y &= b_1 \\ a_{21}\,x + a_{22}\,y &= b_2 \end{aligned} \]

In matrix form this is \(A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}\) with

\[ A=\begin{bmatrix} a_{11} & a_{12} \\ a_{21} & a_{22}\end{bmatrix},\qquad \mathbf{x}=\begin{bmatrix} x \\ y\end{bmatrix},\qquad \mathbf{b}=\begin{bmatrix} b_1 \\ b_2\end{bmatrix}. \]

Method 1 — substitution

  1. Solve one equation for one variable (pick the easiest one).
  2. Plug that expression into the other equation. Now you have one equation in one unknown.
  3. Solve it, then substitute back to get the second unknown.
  4. Check by putting both numbers into the original equations.

Method 2 — elimination

  1. Multiply one (or both) equations by a number so that one variable has equal and opposite coefficients.
  2. Add the equations to cancel that variable.
  3. Solve the resulting one-variable equation, then back-substitute.
  4. Check.

Tiny worked example (substitution)

\[ \begin{aligned} x + y &= 5 \\ x - y &= 1 \end{aligned} \]

From the first equation, \(x = 5 - y\). Put this into the second:

\[ (5 - y) - y = 1 \;\Longrightarrow\; 5 - 2y = 1 \;\Longrightarrow\; 2y = 4 \;\Longrightarrow\; y = 2. \]

Then \(x = 5 - y = 5 - 2 = 3\). So \(x = 3,\ y = 2\).

Check: \(3 + 2 = 5\) ✓ and \(3 - 2 = 1\) ✓.

Drills

Solve each system. For drills 1–8 use substitution. For drills 9–12 use elimination. Drills 13–16 are a mix — solve them whichever way you like, then try the other method too. Always write your steps and finish with a check.

1. \[ \begin{aligned} x &= 4 \\ y &= 7 \end{aligned} \]

2. \[ \begin{aligned} x &= 2 \\ x + y &= 9 \end{aligned} \]

3. \[ \begin{aligned} y &= 5 \\ x - y &= 1 \end{aligned} \]

4. \[ \begin{aligned} x + y &= 6 \\ y &= 2 \end{aligned} \]

5. \[ \begin{aligned} x + y &= 10 \\ x - y &= 2 \end{aligned} \]

6. \[ \begin{aligned} x + y &= 8 \\ x &= 3 \end{aligned} \]

7. \[ \begin{aligned} 2x &= 10 \\ x + y &= 7 \end{aligned} \]

8. \[ \begin{aligned} x + 2y &= 11 \\ y &= 4 \end{aligned} \]

9. \[ \begin{aligned} x + y &= 7 \\ x - y &= 3 \end{aligned} \]

10. \[ \begin{aligned} 2x + y &= 8 \\ x - y &= 1 \end{aligned} \]

11. \[ \begin{aligned} 3x + 2y &= 12 \\ x - 2y &= 4 \end{aligned} \]

12. \[ \begin{aligned} 2x + 3y &= 13 \\ 2x - y &= 5 \end{aligned} \]

13. \[ \begin{aligned} 3x + y &= 10 \\ x + 2y &= 5 \end{aligned} \]

14. \[ \begin{aligned} 2x + 3y &= 8 \\ 3x - y &= 1 \end{aligned} \]

15. \[ \begin{aligned} 4x + 2y &= 5 \\ 2x - y &= 1 \end{aligned} \]

16. \[ \begin{aligned} 3x + 4y &= 2 \\ 5x - 2y &= 12 \end{aligned} \]

Check in R

You can verify any answer with solve. You build the coefficient matrix A (filled by column with byrow = FALSE, or row-by-row with byrow = TRUE) and the right-hand side b, then call solve(A, b).

Drill 5:

A <- matrix(c(1, 1,
              1, -1), nrow = 2, byrow = TRUE)
b <- c(10, 2)
solve(A, b)
#> [1] 6 4      # x = 6, y = 4

Drill 13:

A <- matrix(c(3, 1,
              1, 2), nrow = 2, byrow = TRUE)
b <- c(10, 5)
solve(A, b)
#> [1] 3 1      # x = 3, y = 1

Hints

Hint 1 — drills 1–4

These are almost solved already. One equation hands you a variable directly; just substitute it into the other and read off the second variable. No real algebra needed.

Hint 2 — substitution drills 5–8

Find the equation that is easiest to rearrange into “\(x = \dots\)” or “\(y = \dots\)”. In drill 5, add the two equations or solve \(x + y = 10\) for \(x = 10 - y\) and substitute. In drills 7 and 8 one equation gives a single variable in one step (\(2x = 10 \Rightarrow x = 5\); \(y = 4\)).

Hint 3 — elimination drills 9–12

Line the equations up. If a variable already has matching coefficients with opposite signs (like \(+y\) and \(-y\)), just add the equations to cancel it. In drill 11 the \(y\)-terms are \(+2y\) and \(-2y\) — add to remove \(y\) at once.

Hint 4 — drills 13–16

You may need to multiply an equation by a constant first so a variable cancels. In drill 13, multiply the second equation by 3 to match the \(3x\), then subtract. Answers can be simple fractions (drills 15 and 16) — that is fine, just keep them as exact fractions.

Step-by-step solutions

Solutions

Drill 1

Given directly: \(x = 4\), \(y = 7\). Check: nothing to combine — both values are stated. \(x = 4,\ y = 7\).

Drill 2

From the first equation \(x = 2\). Substitute into \(x + y = 9\): \[2 + y = 9 \;\Rightarrow\; y = 7.\] Answer: \(x = 2,\ y = 7\). Check: \(2 + 7 = 9\) ✓.

Drill 3

From the first equation \(y = 5\). Substitute into \(x - y = 1\): \[x - 5 = 1 \;\Rightarrow\; x = 6.\] Answer: \(x = 6,\ y = 5\). Check: \(6 - 5 = 1\) ✓.

Drill 4

From the second equation \(y = 2\). Substitute into \(x + y = 6\): \[x + 2 = 6 \;\Rightarrow\; x = 4.\] Answer: \(x = 4,\ y = 2\). Check: \(4 + 2 = 6\) ✓.

Drill 5

Solve the first for \(x\): \(x = 10 - y\). Substitute into \(x - y = 2\): \[(10 - y) - y = 2 \;\Rightarrow\; 10 - 2y = 2 \;\Rightarrow\; 2y = 8 \;\Rightarrow\; y = 4.\] Then \(x = 10 - 4 = 6\). Answer: \(x = 6,\ y = 4\). Check: \(6 + 4 = 10\) ✓ and \(6 - 4 = 2\) ✓.

Drill 6

From the second equation \(x = 3\). Substitute into \(x + y = 8\): \[3 + y = 8 \;\Rightarrow\; y = 5.\] Answer: \(x = 3,\ y = 5\). Check: \(3 + 5 = 8\) ✓.

Drill 7

From the first equation \(2x = 10 \Rightarrow x = 5\). Substitute into \(x + y = 7\): \[5 + y = 7 \;\Rightarrow\; y = 2.\] Answer: \(x = 5,\ y = 2\). Check: \(5 + 2 = 7\) ✓.

Drill 8

From the second equation \(y = 4\). Substitute into \(x + 2y = 11\): \[x + 2(4) = 11 \;\Rightarrow\; x + 8 = 11 \;\Rightarrow\; x = 3.\] Answer: \(x = 3,\ y = 4\). Check: \(3 + 2(4) = 3 + 8 = 11\) ✓.

Drill 9 (elimination)

\[ \begin{aligned} x + y &= 7 \\ x - y &= 3 \end{aligned} \] The \(y\)-terms are \(+y\) and \(-y\). Add the two equations (\(R_1 + R_2\)): \[2x = 10 \;\Rightarrow\; x = 5.\] Back-substitute into \(x + y = 7\): \(5 + y = 7 \Rightarrow y = 2\). Answer: \(x = 5,\ y = 2\). Check: \(5 + 2 = 7\) ✓ and \(5 - 2 = 3\) ✓.

Drill 10 (elimination)

\[ \begin{aligned} 2x + y &= 8 \\ x - y &= 1 \end{aligned} \] The \(y\)-terms are \(+y\) and \(-y\). Add (\(R_1 + R_2\)): \[3x = 9 \;\Rightarrow\; x = 3.\] Back-substitute into \(x - y = 1\): \(3 - y = 1 \Rightarrow y = 2\). Answer: \(x = 3,\ y = 2\). Check: \(2(3) + 2 = 8\) ✓ and \(3 - 2 = 1\) ✓.

Drill 11 (elimination)

\[ \begin{aligned} 3x + 2y &= 12 \\ x - 2y &= 4 \end{aligned} \] The \(y\)-terms are \(+2y\) and \(-2y\). Add (\(R_1 + R_2\)): \[4x = 16 \;\Rightarrow\; x = 4.\] Back-substitute into \(x - 2y = 4\): \(4 - 2y = 4 \Rightarrow -2y = 0 \Rightarrow y = 0\). Answer: \(x = 4,\ y = 0\). Check: \(3(4) + 2(0) = 12\) ✓ and \(4 - 2(0) = 4\) ✓.

Drill 12 (elimination)

\[ \begin{aligned} 2x + 3y &= 13 \\ 2x - y &= 5 \end{aligned} \] The \(x\)-terms match (\(2x\) and \(2x\)). Subtract (\(R_1 - R_2\)) to cancel \(x\): \[(3y) - (-y) = 13 - 5 \;\Rightarrow\; 4y = 8 \;\Rightarrow\; y = 2.\] Back-substitute into \(2x - y = 5\): \(2x - 2 = 5 \Rightarrow 2x = 7 \Rightarrow x = \tfrac{7}{2}\). Answer: \(x = \tfrac{7}{2},\ y = 2\). Check: \(2(\tfrac{7}{2}) + 3(2) = 7 + 6 = 13\) ✓ and \(2(\tfrac{7}{2}) - 2 = 7 - 2 = 5\) ✓.

Drill 13 (both methods)

\[ \begin{aligned} 3x + y &= 10 \\ x + 2y &= 5 \end{aligned} \] Elimination: multiply the first equation by 2 so the \(y\)-terms match: \(6x + 2y = 20\). Subtract the second equation: \[(6x + 2y) - (x + 2y) = 20 - 5 \;\Rightarrow\; 5x = 15 \;\Rightarrow\; x = 3.\] Back-substitute into \(3x + y = 10\): \(9 + y = 10 \Rightarrow y = 1\).

Substitution (cross-check): from \(3x + y = 10\), \(y = 10 - 3x\). Put into \(x + 2y = 5\): \[x + 2(10 - 3x) = 5 \Rightarrow x + 20 - 6x = 5 \Rightarrow -5x = -15 \Rightarrow x = 3,\ y = 1.\] Answer: \(x = 3,\ y = 1\). Check: \(3(3) + 1 = 10\) ✓ and \(3 + 2(1) = 5\) ✓.

Drill 14 (both methods)

\[ \begin{aligned} 2x + 3y &= 8 \\ 3x - y &= 1 \end{aligned} \] Elimination: multiply the second equation by 3 so the \(y\)-terms become \(-3y\): \(9x - 3y = 3\). Add to the first: \[(2x + 3y) + (9x - 3y) = 8 + 3 \;\Rightarrow\; 11x = 11 \;\Rightarrow\; x = 1.\] Back-substitute into \(3x - y = 1\): \(3 - y = 1 \Rightarrow y = 2\).

Substitution (cross-check): from \(3x - y = 1\), \(y = 3x - 1\). Put into \(2x + 3y = 8\): \[2x + 3(3x - 1) = 8 \Rightarrow 2x + 9x - 3 = 8 \Rightarrow 11x = 11 \Rightarrow x = 1,\ y = 2.\] Answer: \(x = 1,\ y = 2\). Check: \(2(1) + 3(2) = 8\) ✓ and \(3(1) - 2 = 1\) ✓.

Drill 15 (both methods)

\[ \begin{aligned} 4x + 2y &= 5 \\ 2x - y &= 1 \end{aligned} \] Elimination: multiply the second equation by 2 so the \(y\)-terms become \(-2y\): \(4x - 2y = 2\). Add to the first: \[(4x + 2y) + (4x - 2y) = 5 + 2 \;\Rightarrow\; 8x = 7 \;\Rightarrow\; x = \tfrac{7}{8}.\] Back-substitute into \(2x - y = 1\): \(2(\tfrac{7}{8}) - y = 1 \Rightarrow \tfrac{7}{4} - y = 1 \Rightarrow y = \tfrac{3}{4}\).

Substitution (cross-check): from \(2x - y = 1\), \(y = 2x - 1\). Put into \(4x + 2y = 5\): \[4x + 2(2x - 1) = 5 \Rightarrow 4x + 4x - 2 = 5 \Rightarrow 8x = 7 \Rightarrow x = \tfrac{7}{8},\ y = \tfrac{3}{4}.\] Answer: \(x = \tfrac{7}{8},\ y = \tfrac{3}{4}\). Check: \(4(\tfrac{7}{8}) + 2(\tfrac{3}{4}) = \tfrac{7}{2} + \tfrac{3}{2} = 5\) ✓ and \(2(\tfrac{7}{8}) - \tfrac{3}{4} = \tfrac{7}{4} - \tfrac{3}{4} = 1\) ✓.

Drill 16 (both methods)

\[ \begin{aligned} 3x + 4y &= 2 \\ 5x - 2y &= 12 \end{aligned} \] Elimination: multiply the second equation by 2 so the \(y\)-terms become \(-4y\): \(10x - 4y = 24\). Add to the first: \[(3x + 4y) + (10x - 4y) = 2 + 24 \;\Rightarrow\; 13x = 26 \;\Rightarrow\; x = 2.\] Back-substitute into \(3x + 4y = 2\): \(6 + 4y = 2 \Rightarrow 4y = -4 \Rightarrow y = -1\).

Substitution (cross-check): from \(5x - 2y = 12\), \(2y = 5x - 12 \Rightarrow y = \tfrac{5x - 12}{2}\). Put into \(3x + 4y = 2\): \[3x + 4\cdot\tfrac{5x - 12}{2} = 2 \Rightarrow 3x + 2(5x - 12) = 2 \Rightarrow 13x - 24 = 2 \Rightarrow x = 2,\ y = -1.\] Answer: \(x = 2,\ y = -1\). Check: \(3(2) + 4(-1) = 6 - 4 = 2\) ✓ and \(5(2) - 2(-1) = 10 + 2 = 12\) ✓.
