Quiz: eigenvalues & eigenvectors

Short questions to test your understanding of eigenvalues and eigenvectors. Try each on paper first, then open the answer. Most are in the style of the on-campus exam (no computer).

Q1. In one sentence: what makes a vector \(\mathbf{v}\) an eigenvector of a matrix \(A\)?

Hint

Think about how the direction of \(\mathbf{v}\) changes (or doesn’t) when you apply \(A\).

Answer

\(A\) only scales \(\mathbf{v}\) — it stays on the same line through the origin — instead of rotating it onto a new direction. Formally \(A\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v}\) for some scalar \(\lambda\) (with \(\mathbf{v}\neq\mathbf{0}\)).

Q2. \(A\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 \\ 9 \end{pmatrix}\) and \(\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}\). Is \(\mathbf{v}\) an eigenvector? If so, what is \(\lambda\)?

Hint

Is the output a scalar multiple of \(\mathbf{v}\)?

Answer

\((6,9) = 3(2,3)\), so yes — \(\mathbf{v}\) is an eigenvector with \(\lambda = 3\).

Q3. Write down the matrix \(A - \lambda I\) for \(A = \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 2 \\ 1 & 4 \end{pmatrix}\).

Hint

Subtract \(\lambda\) from the diagonal only.

Answer

\(\begin{pmatrix} 5-\lambda & 2 \\ 1 & 4-\lambda \end{pmatrix}\) — off-diagonals unchanged.

Q4. What is the characteristic equation, and what is it used for?

Hint

It comes from requiring a nonzero \(\mathbf{v}\) with \((A-\lambda I)\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}\).

Answer

\(\det(A - \lambda I) = 0\). Solving it (a quadratic for a \(2\times2\)) gives the eigenvalues. A nonzero \(\mathbf{v}\) can only be sent to \(\mathbf{0}\) when \(A-\lambda I\) is singular, i.e. its determinant is zero.

Q5. Find the eigenvalues of \(A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 7 \end{pmatrix}\) by inspection.

Hint

For a diagonal matrix the eigenvalues are sitting right there.

Answer

\(\lambda = 2\) and \(\lambda = 7\) — the diagonal entries. (Eigenvectors are the axes \((1,0)\) and \((0,1)\).)

Q6. Solve \(\det(A - \lambda I) = 0\) for \(A = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 2 \\ 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}\).

Hint

\((3-\lambda)^2 - 4 = 0\). Factor or take square roots.

Answer

\((3-\lambda)^2 = 4 \Rightarrow 3-\lambda = \pm 2 \Rightarrow \lambda = 1\) or \(\lambda = 5\). (Check: trace \(6 = 1+5\), det \(9-4=5 = 1\cdot5\).)

Q7. Your matrix has trace 7 and determinant 12. One eigenvalue is 3. What is the other?

Hint

Sum of eigenvalues = trace; product = determinant.

Answer

The other is \(7 - 3 = 4\) (and \(3\times4 = 12\) confirms it). Eigenvalues 3 and 4.

Q8. For \(A = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 0 & 4 \end{pmatrix}\) (eigenvalue \(\lambda = 4\), repeated), find an eigenvector.

Hint

Solve \((A - 4I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}\). What does the top row force?

Answer

\(A - 4I = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\). Row 1 says \(v_2 = 0\), with \(v_1\) free, so \(\mathbf{v} = (1, 0)\). (Note: even though \(\lambda=4\) is repeated, this matrix has only one eigen-direction — it is not symmetric.)

Q9. Why is a symmetric matrix nice? Give the two guarantees.

Hint

Spectral theorem — think about realness and angles.

Answer
  1. All eigenvalues are real (never complex). (2) Eigenvectors for different eigenvalues are orthogonal. This is why covariance matrices give clean perpendicular axes of variation.

Q10. A pure rotation \(\begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\) has no real eigenvector. Explain why, geometrically.

Hint

What does a \(90^\circ\) rotation do to every direction?

Answer

A \(90^\circ\) rotation moves every vector onto a new direction, so no nonzero real vector comes back as a multiple of itself. The characteristic equation \(\lambda^2 + 1 = 0\) has no real root, confirming this.

Q11. A covariance matrix has eigenvalues \(\lambda_1 = 12\) (eigenvector \(\mathbf{v}_1\)) and \(\lambda_2 = 2\) (eigenvector \(\mathbf{v}_2\)). Along which direction does the data vary most, and how much variance is there along the perpendicular direction?

Hint

The eigenvalue measures the variance captured along its eigenvector.

Answer

The data vary most along \(\mathbf{v}_1\) (largest eigenvalue, 12) — the long axis of the cloud. Along the perpendicular direction \(\mathbf{v}_2\) the variance is 2. (This is exactly the idea behind the first principal component.)

Q12. True or false: if \(\mathbf{v}\) is an eigenvector of \(A\), then so is \(-5\mathbf{v}\), with the same eigenvalue.

Hint

\(A(-5\mathbf{v}) = -5(A\mathbf{v})\).

Answer

True. \(A(-5\mathbf{v}) = -5(A\mathbf{v}) = -5(\lambda\mathbf{v}) = \lambda(-5\mathbf{v})\). Any nonzero multiple of an eigenvector is an eigenvector with the same eigenvalue — an eigenvector really specifies a whole line (a direction).
