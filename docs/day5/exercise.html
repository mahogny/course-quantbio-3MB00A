Exercise: eigenvalues & eigenvectors

Goal

By the end of this exercise you should be able to:

  • explain what makes an eigenvector special (\(A\) only scales it, never rotates it),
  • write and read the eigen relation \(A\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v}\),
  • find the eigenvalues of a \(2\times2\) matrix by hand from the characteristic equation \(\det(A-\lambda I)=0\),
  • find the matching eigenvector for each eigenvalue, and verify it,
  • use the trace/determinant checks to catch arithmetic slips,
  • explain why a symmetric matrix (such as a covariance matrix) has real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors.

This is a concept day and the by-hand recipe is examined directly. Spend most of your time on the pen-and-paper problems; the R section is only to confirm your hand answers (full R fluency comes tomorrow).

Background

A nonzero vector \(\mathbf{v}\) is an eigenvector of \(A\) with eigenvalue \(\lambda\) when

\[A\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v}.\]

The matrix acts on that one direction like a plain number \(\lambda\): it stretches (\(|\lambda|>1\)), shrinks (\(|\lambda|<1\)), or flips (\(\lambda<0\)) the vector, but never knocks it off its line.

The by-hand recipe (memorise this):

  1. Form \(A - \lambda I\) by subtracting \(\lambda\) from each diagonal entry: \[A - \lambda I = \begin{pmatrix} a-\lambda & b \\ c & d-\lambda \end{pmatrix}\]
  2. Set its determinant to zero (the characteristic equation). For a \(2\times2\), \(\det\begin{pmatrix}p&q\\r&s\end{pmatrix} = ps - qr\), so \[(a-\lambda)(d-\lambda) - bc = 0,\] a quadratic in \(\lambda\). Solve it for the eigenvalues.
  3. For each \(\lambda\), solve \((A-\lambda I)\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}\) to get the eigenvector. Any one row gives the relation between \(v_1\) and \(v_2\); pick the simplest integers.
  4. Verify \(A\mathbf{v}=\lambda\mathbf{v}\), and check $= $ trace and \(\prod\lambda = \det A\).

Symmetric matrices (\(A = A^\top\), e.g. any covariance matrix) always have real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors.

By hand

B1. For \(A = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\), find the eigenvalues by inspection (it is diagonal). What are the eigenvectors? What does \(A\) do geometrically?

B2. For \(A = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\), set up and solve \(\det(A-\lambda I)=0\). (Hint: it factors nicely; a triangular matrix shows its eigenvalues on the diagonal.)

B3. For the symmetric matrix \(A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\): (a) find the eigenvalues from the characteristic equation; (b) find an eigenvector for each; (c) verify the two eigenvectors are orthogonal (dot product 0); (d) confirm \(\sum\lambda\) = trace and \(\prod\lambda = \det A\).

B4. For \(A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\), find both eigenvalues. Then try \(A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\), whose quadratic does not factor with whole numbers — use the quadratic formula and leave a square root in the answer.

B5. Show that \(\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}\) is an eigenvector of \(A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}\) by computing \(A\mathbf{v}\) and reading off \(\lambda\). (You do not need the characteristic equation for this — just multiply.)

B6. The rotation matrix \(A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\) (rotate \(90^\circ\)). Set up \(\det(A-\lambda I)=0\) and explain, from the equation, why it has no real eigenvalue. Relate this to what the matrix does geometrically.

B7. (covariance flavour) A small dataset has covariance matrix \(C = \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 4 \\ 4 & 5 \end{pmatrix}\). Find its eigenvalues and eigenvectors by hand. Which eigenvector points along the direction of greatest variation, and what is the variance along it?

In R

Use R only to confirm the hand answers above. (Tomorrow we use eigen() in earnest; today we lean on simple checks.)

Verify an eigen relation directly

A <- matrix(c(2, 1,
              1, 2), nrow = 2, byrow = TRUE)
v <- c(1, 1)          # claimed eigenvector
A %*% v               # 3 3  -> equals 3 * v, so lambda = 3
3 * v                 # 3 3  -> matches
# B5: is (1,2) an eigenvector of this A?
A <- matrix(c(0, 1,
             -2, 3), nrow = 2, byrow = TRUE)
v <- c(1, 2)
A %*% v               # 2 4 = 2 * (1,2) -> yes, lambda = 2

Check eigenvalues with trace and determinant

A <- matrix(c(2, 1,
              1, 2), nrow = 2, byrow = TRUE)
sum(diag(A))          # trace = 4  -> should equal lambda1 + lambda2
det(A)                # det   = 3  -> should equal lambda1 * lambda2
# your hand answers were 3 and 1:  3+1=4, 3*1=3  ->  both check out

Confirm two eigenvectors are orthogonal

v1 <- c(1,  1)        # for lambda = 3
v2 <- c(1, -1)        # for lambda = 1
sum(v1 * v2)          # 0  -> orthogonal (as expected for symmetric A)

Tasks

R1. Using the trace/determinant block, verify your eigenvalues from B3 (you should already know they are 3 and 1).

R2. For B7, build C <- matrix(c(5,4,4,5), 2, byrow = TRUE), then verify your hand eigenvalues with sum(diag(C)) and det(C), and confirm your leading eigenvector v satisfies C %*% v == lambda * v.

R3. Take the rotation matrix from B6, A <- matrix(c(0,-1,1,0), 2, byrow = TRUE). Pick any nonzero vector and compute A %*% v for a few different v. Convince yourself no nonzero real vector comes back as a multiple of itself (every vector is rotated).

Hints

Hint 1 — building A - lambda*I

Subtract \(\lambda\) from only the diagonal entries; the off-diagonals \(b\) and \(c\) are unchanged. Then the characteristic equation is \((a-\lambda)(d-\lambda) - bc = 0\).

Hint 2 — solving the quadratic

\((a-\lambda)(d-\lambda) - bc = 0\) always multiplies out to \(\lambda^2 - (a+d)\lambda + (ad - bc) = 0\). Note \(a+d\) is the trace and \(ad-bc\) is the determinant. Factor it, or use \(\lambda = \dfrac{(a+d) \pm \sqrt{(a+d)^2 - 4(ad-bc)}}{2}\).

Hint 3 — finding the eigenvector

Plug \(\lambda\) into \(A-\lambda I\) and write out the two equations \((A-\lambda I)\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}\). Because the matrix is singular, the two rows say the same thing — use one row to get \(v_2\) in terms of \(v_1\), then pick \(v_1 = 1\) (or whatever gives nice integers).

Hint 4 — the trace/determinant checks

Sum of eigenvalues = trace (sum of diagonal) = sum(diag(A)); product of eigenvalues = determinant = det(A). If your two roots fail either check you made an arithmetic error.

Hint 5 — verifying in R

Build matrices with matrix(..., byrow = TRUE), multiply with %*%, and compare A %*% v against lambda * v. The dot product sum(v1 * v2) should be 0 for orthogonal eigenvectors.

Solutions

Solution

B1. Diagonal matrix → eigenvalues are the diagonal entries: \(\lambda_1 = 4\), \(\lambda_2 = 2\). Eigenvectors are the axes: \((1,0)\) for \(\lambda=4\) and \((0,1)\) for \(\lambda=2\). Geometrically \(A\) stretches the x-axis by 4 and the y-axis by 2.

B2. \(\det\begin{pmatrix}3-\lambda & 1\\ 0 & 2-\lambda\end{pmatrix} = (3-\lambda)(2-\lambda) - 0 = 0\), so \(\lambda = 3\) or \(\lambda = 2\). A triangular matrix shows its eigenvalues on the diagonal.

B3. (a) \((2-\lambda)^2 - 1 = \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 3 = (\lambda-3)(\lambda-1)=0 \Rightarrow \lambda = 3, 1\). (b) For \(\lambda=3\): \(A-3I = \begin{pmatrix}-1&1\\1&-1\end{pmatrix}\), row 1 gives \(-v_1+v_2=0\), so \(\mathbf{v}_1=(1,1)\). For \(\lambda=1\): \(A-I=\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\1&1\end{pmatrix}\), row 1 gives \(v_1+v_2=0\), so \(\mathbf{v}_2=(1,-1)\). (c) \((1)(1)+(1)(-1) = 0\) → orthogonal. ✓ (expected: \(A\) is symmetric). (d) trace \(= 2+2 = 4 = 3+1\) ✓; \(\det = 2\cdot2 - 1\cdot1 = 3 = 3\cdot1\) ✓.

B4. First matrix: \(\det\begin{pmatrix}1-\lambda & 2\\ 2 & 1-\lambda\end{pmatrix} = (1-\lambda)^2 - 4 = \lambda^2 - 2\lambda - 3 = (\lambda-3)(\lambda+1) = 0\), so \(\lambda = 3\) or \(\lambda = -1\). Second matrix: \(\det\begin{pmatrix}2-\lambda & 1\\ 1 & -\lambda\end{pmatrix} = (2-\lambda)(-\lambda) - 1 = \lambda^2 - 2\lambda - 1 = 0\). This does not factor over the integers, so use the formula: \(\lambda = \frac{2 \pm \sqrt{4+4}}{2} = 1 \pm \sqrt{2}\). (Both eigenvalues are real, as they must be for a symmetric matrix.)

B5. \(A\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix}0&1\\-2&3\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1\\2\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}0\cdot1+1\cdot2\\-2\cdot1+3\cdot2\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}2\\4\end{pmatrix} = 2\begin{pmatrix}1\\2\end{pmatrix}\). So \(\mathbf{v}\) is an eigenvector with \(\lambda = 2\).

B6. \(\det\begin{pmatrix}-\lambda & -1\\ 1 & -\lambda\end{pmatrix} = \lambda^2 - (-1)(1) = \lambda^2 + 1 = 0 \Rightarrow \lambda^2 = -1\). No real solution (it needs \(\sqrt{-1}\)). Geometrically a \(90^\circ\) rotation moves every direction, so no real vector comes back as a multiple of itself — hence no real eigenvalue/eigenvector.

B7. \((5-\lambda)^2 - 16 = \lambda^2 - 10\lambda + 9 = (\lambda-9)(\lambda-1) = 0 \Rightarrow \lambda = 9, 1\). For \(\lambda=9\): \(C-9I=\begin{pmatrix}-4&4\\4&-4\end{pmatrix}\) → \(v_2 = v_1\) → \(\mathbf{v}=(1,1)\). For \(\lambda=1\): \(\mathbf{v}=(1,-1)\). The direction of greatest variation is the eigenvector with the largest eigenvalue, \(\mathbf{v}=(1,1)\) with \(\lambda = 9\); the variance along that axis is 9. (Checks: trace \(=10=9+1\), \(\det = 25-16 = 9 = 9\cdot1\).)

R1. sum(diag(A)) returns 4 and det(A) returns 3, matching \(3+1\) and \(3\cdot1\).

R2. sum(diag(C)) is 10 (\(=9+1\)), det(C) is 9 (\(=9\cdot1\)). With v <- c(1,1) and lambda <- 9, C %*% v returns 9 9 \(= 9\cdot(1,1)\).

R3. Each A %*% v is the input rotated \(90^\circ\); e.g. (1,0) -> (0,1), (2,1) -> (-1,2). None lands on the same line as its input, confirming there is no real eigenvector.

Where this leads

  • Day 6 (eigen in R): eigen(A) returns all eigenvalues and (unit-length) eigenvectors at once. You will read its $values and $vectors, sort them, verify \(A\mathbf{v}=\lambda\mathbf{v}\), and reconstruct \(A = V\Lambda V^{-1}\).
  • Covariance & PCA (Week 2): the leading eigenvector of a covariance matrix (largest eigenvalue) is the first principal component — the main axis of variation in your data. B7 was a miniature PCA computation.
  • Day 7–8 (Markov chains): the stationary distribution of a Markov chain is the eigenvector with eigenvalue \(\lambda = 1\) of its transition matrix.
